Luanching Nebuloid

Hello all! Welcome to the my first blog post on Nebuloid. I believe that every developer worth their salt can benefit from having a personal website! But, that is not to say that an absence of a website makes a developer any worse at what they do… Rather, it's simply about advocating for yourself.

A close friend of mine once asked me: if you don't advocate for yourself, who will? If you don't narrorate your story, someone else will do it for you — and it might not be the story you wish to tell.

I feel like this is a relatable experience to any web developer. That said, I'm quite proud to introduce my developer blog & portfolio, Nebuloid.

Why Nebuloid?

The website's name may seem strange to you. Why give it a name like Nebuloid? Why give it a name at all?

Well, I wanted to introduce whimsy to my professional appearance — it is a manifestation of who I am. I believe that, without whimsy, without a little humor or personality, we can lose ourselves and our work in society's identity of corporate culture. We lose the ability to remain creative, aspirational, driven, passionate… So, inversely, to bring our authentic selves to work, we are giving it our all and are in the mindset to truly impact the future of a product.

Technologies

That's the philosophy of the site, but what about the technologies that power it? When I set out to build this website, I wanted to be the owner of the website, from the rock-bottom to the tippy-top of the stack. I created a Backend API and a Frontend website for it, set up a Linux Server and a DB Cluster, using all of these technologies:

  • TypeScript
  • ESLint
  • NodeJS
  • ExpressJS
  • GraphQL
  • Mongoose
  • MongoDB
  • Linux Server (Ubuntu)
  • SSH
  • NGINX
  • PM2
  • NextJS
  • ReactJS
  • SASS
  • Figma
  • StoryBook

Lovingly, I collectively call this the GREMLiN Stack. Okay, that's a made up acronym, but it sounds pretty good, right?

Creating something like this, and owning it throughout the stack, really helped me grasp a whole onslaught of topics. I hadn't had much experience with Figma or with Server Administration, for example.

As a Full-Stack developer, I believe its important to understand the entirety of a project's stack on some level. While people are right to declare a niche of their work as a highlighted expertise, at times its just as important to understand the connecting areas of a system. And while I maintain this philosophy, I also just think its fun to see everything connect smoothly and seamlessly. That, my friends, is why I am a Full-Stack engineer, and not specifically a Frontend or Backend engineer.
